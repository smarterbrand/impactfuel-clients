01· Market Position

An advisor. Not a transaction.

You lead with three words: corporate real estate advisor. Not “broker,” not “agent.” The label is the strategy — it signals counsel over commission and sets up everything else in this memo.

What you want people to understand

  • Advisor, not broker. You’re paid on commission, but the job is advising — reading the financials and the debt behind a deal so it works as well as possible for your client, whether they’re buying, leasing, or recovering an asset.
  • Relationship-first, by design. You treat every deal like your own regardless of size, work at the pace the deal deserves, and will tell a client not to do a deal when your gut says it’s unsound. You’re comfortable not being the right fit for people who only want speed.
  • The center of gravity is shifting. A niche you built resolving distressed debt for banks is becoming your main business — now spanning financial analysis, management services, and front-end advisory, with brokerage as one piece. This is where the marketing should point.

Where you sit in the market

Two axes capture your core differentiation: how transactional vs. advisory the work is, and how volume- vs. relationship-driven the practice is. You’re deliberately in the corner almost nobody else occupies.

02· Best-Fit Audience

Who this is for.

Two audiences, one filter. The content should pre-qualify — pulling the right people in and letting the wrong ones quietly self-select out.

Financial institutions with distressed CRE debt

The special-asset, asset-resolution, and workout officers inside banks like PNC. They call when an asset has gone sideways and they need one trusted person to step in, preserve the collateral, and sell it.

What pushes them to call:

  • Borrower fraud
  • Property neglect & deferred maintenance
  • Over-leveraged deals handed back to the lender
  • An owner who has died, fallen ill, or simply walked away
The relationship base across asset classes

Buyers, tenants, sellers, and investors across the greater Pittsburgh market who value honest counsel and a long relationship over a fast transaction — the clients who remember you at the next lease or sale, sometimes 10–15 years later.

This base keeps your lights on and feeds referrals. The marketing won’t lead here, but it should never alienate it.

The one thing that decides fit

Alignment on communication and pace. The right client wants an advisor who’ll be honest about timeline and risk. The wrong client wants a sign in the yard and a closing in 45 days.

Why it matters

Misaligned engagements burn money — marketing spent up front, then a relationship that breaks before closing. Screening for fit isn’t fussiness; it’s economics. The named buyer profiles come in the next session (the Ideal Client Profile).

05· Core Point of View

Things worth saying out loud.

The honest, slightly uncomfortable truths that make content memorable. Each one is raw material for the editorial calendar — expand for the angle.

CRE isn’t residential — and pretending otherwise sets everyone up to fail.

Deals don’t close in 45 days. The business is slow and inefficient by nature: architects need six to eight weeks, building permits 30–60 days, construction 90+ — and almost none of it is inside a broker’s control. A client can fall in love with a building in 24 hours and still wait 120 days to occupy it.

Setting a realistic timeline up front is the advisor’s real job. Most won’t do it because clients don’t want to hear it.

◆ Content angle · Strong candidate for Post #1
Pittsburgh is a shrinking market — brokers are recycling tenants, not winning growth.

Here, the game is largely moving existing tenants from one building to another, not capturing net-new demand the way an exploding market like Dallas does. It’s not good for the brokers, and it’s not good for the city.

It’s an uncomfortable truth — which is exactly why it lands the moment it’s said by someone credible enough to say it.

◆ Content angle · The local truth-teller post
The office reckoning is already visible downtown.

A landmark tower like BNY Mellon — roughly 1.6M square feet, near 10% of downtown office1 — risks going dark, with no single anchor tenant large enough to fill it. It’s a bellwether for where downtown is headed.

◆ Content angle · Market-watch / contrarian forecast
The next few years favor the fixer, not the dealmaker.

Higher interest rates are turning over over-leveraged deals, and you anticipate a wave of distressed inventory from late 2026 through 2028 and beyond. It’s work that scales in any market — and gets busiest precisely when everyone else is hurting.

The line that started it all

Martin Perry is the commercial real estate advisor you call when you need a true advisor in challenging times — or facing headwinds most brokers don’t know how to navigate.

Claims to avoid

  1. No fabrication, ever. Only deals you genuinely closed or worked on. No invented stories, no borrowed credit.
  2. Don’t claim asset-class specialization you don’t want to own. Frame the breadth as deliberate — “knowledgeable across classes, expert at getting deals done here” — rather than overstating depth in any one niche.
  3. Don’t overpromise speed or certainty. The whole brand rests on honest timelines; the messaging must never undercut that.
