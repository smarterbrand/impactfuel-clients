01· How it works

One conversation.
Four pieces.

The engine is simple: Martin determines what's worth talking about, we design the questions, record the conversation, and produce everything from it — AI trained on his voice and positioning.

The core principle

You determine what's worth talking about and who knows the most about it. You schedule the interview — every week. We do the rest: questionnaire design, recording, content creation, distribution.

The goal is an 800–1,200 word post that sounds exactly like Martin talking to a bank officer over coffee. One idea, one reader, one next step.

01.

Set the frame (Monday)

Martin answers 9 questions in ~5 minutes mid-week, before the interview. Not writing the post — just answering honestly, the way he'd explain it to a client across the table. Who is this for? What's the one point? What's the contrarian truth?

02.

Record the interview

30-minute conversation, recorded. Martin talks; we listen and guide. The recording is the raw material for everything — blog post, email, LinkedIn excerpts, and eventually a short-form video.

03.

Produce the content

AI trained on Martin's voice and guardrails drafts the post, email, and three LinkedIn excerpts from the recording. Impact Fuel reviews against the voice profile before anything leaves our hands.

04.

Review & approve

Martin reviews the draft. One round of edits, then we schedule and publish. The rule: if it doesn't sound like something he'd say to a client's face, it doesn't go out.

05.

Distribute

Blog post goes live. Email goes to his list. Three LinkedIn excerpts post across the week — one observation, one story, one list — from the same source material.

02· Monthly Cadence

The monthly rhythm.

Every month runs on the same four-week framework. Predictable for Martin, manageable for us, consistent for his audience.

Week 1
Plan the month

Pick the topic and the person who knows the most about it. Send the invite and topic questionnaire so they can prepare.

Week 2
Record the interview

Have and record the conversation. ~30 minutes. Simple setup — record on your meeting app or place a phone on the table.

Week 3
Create the content

We produce the blog post, email, three social posts, and optionally a short podcast clip from the recording — with AI assistance, reviewed by Impact Fuel.

Week 4
Publish & post

Post the blog and send the email. LinkedIn excerpts go out across the week. Engage with Martin's network throughout the month.

Scale path

Martin starts at monthly (4 posts). As the engine matures and the audience responds, the cadence can move to bi-weekly and eventually weekly — using the same interview format, just more often. The process doesn't change; only the frequency does.

03· The Post Brief

Nine questions.
One great post.

Martin answers these before every interview. Takes ~5 minutes. The answers become the spine of the conversation and the brief for the draft. The golden rule: one post, one idea, one reader, one next step. If an answer splits into two, you've got two posts — bank the second one.

Part 1 · Set the frame

  • Q1

    Who is this for? Pick ONE. The Lender (special-assets officer, chief credit officer, creditors'-rights attorney) or The Owner / Investor (family office, HNW buyer, nonprofit or corporate occupier). Name the actual person in your head — "the head of special assets at a regional bank who's three years into a loan that never paid" beats "lenders."

  • Q2

    What problem are they facing right now that this post helps with? In their words, not industry jargon. What's keeping this person up this week? Lender example: "We've absorbed bad loans from acquisitions we never underwrote." Owner example: "There's a deal I could buy at a discount but I'm on a 45-day clock and I'm not ready."

  • Q3

    What's the ONE point I'm making? One sentence. The whole post defends this and nothing else.

  • Q4

    What's the contrarian truth — the thing only I'd say out loud? The industry line you'd push back on. No POV = no reason to read. e.g., "Patience is a competitive advantage." "Extend-and-pretend is over." "You don't have to do the deal — your client does."

Part 2 · Make it stick

  • Q5

    What's the one thing I want them to remember a week later? The line they could repeat to a colleague. Write it as you'd actually say it.

  • Q6

    What real example proves it? A deal, a moment, a mistake you saw firsthand. Real numbers when you have them — before → after, and how long it took. Never a borrower's, client's, or bank's name. Never a deal you weren't part of.

  • Q7

    What does this look like when it goes wrong? The cost of ignoring the point. e.g., the buyer who called on day 42 of a 45-day exchange; the bank that waited three years; the third retail location that should never have opened.

Part 3 · The ask

  • Q8

    What do I want them to do next? ONE next step, matched to this reader. Not "call me." A specific, useful action. e.g., "If you're 12–18 months from a maturity, ask your team one question: are we firefighting or fireproofing?" Then the soft line: that's the conversation I'm built for.

  • Q9

    Who should read this and opt OUT? Name the wrong-fit reader. Saying who this isn't for is how you earn trust with who it is for. e.g., small-retail bargain-hunters, anyone who wants a sign in the yard and offers next week.

04· What we produce

One interview.
Four outputs.

Every recorded interview produces four pieces of content — all from the same source material, all in Martin's voice.

From every interview
  • Blog post (800–1,200 words) — The full argument. Hook → Problem → Point → Proof → So what → Close. Published on martintypeplace.com and shared in email.
  • Email to the list — A shorter version of the post, or a "here's what I published this week" wrapper with the key pull-quote. Goes to the full list.
  • LinkedIn: The observation post — Martin's Q4 contrarian truth, standalone. Short, specific, a point of view anyone in CRE should have seen but no one's said yet.
  • LinkedIn: The story post — The Q6 example, told in 3–5 short paragraphs. Real deal, anonymized, with the mechanics of what he did step by step.
  • LinkedIn: The list post — 3 sub-points drawn from inside the post. "3 things banks wait too long to do." "2 reasons the office reckoning is worse than people think." Self-writing once the post exists.
LinkedIn cadence

Three LinkedIn excerpts post across the week — spaced out, not all at once. The same week's post fuels the whole LinkedIn week. Martin only needs to look at one thing, once: the original interview.

05· Guardrails

What never goes out.

Every piece of content — post, email, or LinkedIn — must pass these before it publishes.

Rule 1
No fabrication, ever.
Only deals Martin genuinely closed or worked on. No invented stories, no borrowed credit, no statistics he didn't generate. A hard line — and a credibility asset.
Rule 2
No fake or artificial urgency.
"Best and final," invented deadlines, manufactured scarcity. Martin's whole brand rests on honest timelines — the messaging can never undercut that.
Rule 3
No disparaging borrowers, clients, or any party.
Respectful and tactful, always — even when describing a situation that went badly. The business lives on relationships and referrals. That includes how we write about every party in every deal.
Rule 4
No hype, no ego, no "best in the market."
Nothing he wouldn't say to a client's face. These phrases would make him cringe in a real conversation — they don't belong on the page either. One idea, one reader, one next step.
Rule 5
Never claim a deal you weren't in.
If you can't share numbers, share the mechanics — what you did, step by step. The specificity is more persuasive than any claimed result anyway.
